What Fires the Forests?

Forest fires have a significant and profound influence on the ecology of one place. It would also cause great economic loss and even threaten people's safety. An analysis of the potential factors of forest fire may help us to control and prevent it in the future. The goal of the following visualizations is to present how location, time, and some attributes of the trees are related to the break out of a forest fire. Our database is the forest fire and tree cover record of the United States from 1992 to 2015.

Season & Region

Season possesses a strong influence on the number of forest fires. Overall, forest fire happens most frequently in March and July. In the summer time, especially from June to August, lightning always causes more forest fires due to the frequently visited thundershowers.

In terms of geographical distribution, California, Georgia, and Texas are the 3 states with the most forest fires. If we draw closer to the geographic distribution, we will discover that the fire occurrences show different patterns in the east and the west of United States. In eastern US, the peak of forest fire occurs in March and the main cause is debris burning and arson. However, on the west coast of US, forest fires occur most frequently in July and the chief offender is lightning. Besides, there are also other factors that assist the forest fires, namely, temperature and moisture. Around the year, California collects most of its moisture in fall and winter times. In summer, the plants turn dry and the temperature is high. These two conditions increase the probability of forest fires.

Reasoning

The differences in cause factor sometimes is the result of different state laws. For exmple, Missouri and Illinois are near and similar in area, but in Missouri there are 7635 wildfires caused by debris burning while there are only 583 in Illinois. We find that it's strictly prohibited in Illinois to burn debris while in Missouri, debris burning in some situations is legal. Thus debris burning happens less often in Illinois, resulting fewer wildfires caused by debris burning.

Tree Density

One may think that tree density is an important factor that could determine the likelihood of a forest fire. We can select one or more types of trees in the following chart and see their distributed areas across the US to explore the relationship between tree types and their geographical growth conditions. Conversely, we can also select one or more states and see the types of trees growing in that area. This can help us determine which types of tree are likely to cause a fire.

On the whole, we plot the density of fires and trees in all US states to roughly estimate any existence of similar patterns between tree density and fire occurrences. In comparison, we could say that fire density does have some partial influence on forest fires. Tree density could explain the high frequency of forest fires in southeast America and the relatively low frequency in northwest America. However, tree density and fire density appear to have opposite relationship on the west coast or in the northeast area.

Moreover, we cannot even take the density distribution of a specific type of tree as indication for forest fire indicator. For example, if we select "California" in US Tree Density Map, we will find that Douglas-fir, Ponderosa pine and lodgepone pine are the species with largest quantities within the state. However, if we select the specific tree types, we will discover that California is not the states with the most trees of this species.

Therefore, To properly explain the frequent emergence of forest fires on places such as the west coast, we need to find other supportive evidence.

Tree Height

After massive amount of experiments, we discover that tree height seems to have a more direct relationship with the occurrence of fires. To support this proposition, we compute the average height of trees in each state and rank the type of trees according to their average heights. We can select one or more types of trees in the following graph and see their distribution across the country.

Surprisingly, we can find that California takes over the top few species of trees. The trees in the west is significant higher than the trees in the east, which makes them more possible to be struck by lightning and burn.

Conclusion

In this article, we discuss several potential attributes of trees that might affect the occurrence of forest fires. We discover that while the density of trees could partially explain the problem, forest fires also possess a strong relationship with the height of trees. With this idea in mind, the next time when you happen to see some tall trees being cut in a national park, please don't be too sad. This can actually help to preserve more trees.
